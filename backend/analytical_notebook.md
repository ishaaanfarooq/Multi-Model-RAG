## [2026-04-23 21:26:32] Analysis: Why is the sky blue?

### Synthesis
The sky appears blue due to a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering, named after the British physicist Lord Rayleigh, who first described it in the late 19th century. This process occurs when sunlight enters Earth's atmosphere and interacts with the tiny molecules of gases such as nitrogen and oxygen. The shorter (blue) wavelengths of light are scattered more than the longer (red) wavelengths by these molecules, resulting in the blue color we see in the sky.

In more technical terms, when sunlight hits a molecule of gas in the atmosphere, it causes the molecule to vibrate. These vibrations scatter the light in all directions, but they scatter shorter wavelengths (like blue and violet) more than longer wavelengths (like red and orange). As a result, our eyes see more of the scattered blue light coming from all directions, which is why the sky appears blue.

This phenomenon is responsible for many of the colorful effects we see in nature, such as sunsets and clouds, but it's most pronounced when the sun is overhead and the atmosphere is clear. Under an overcast sky, there is no direct sunlight, and all light results from diffused skylight radiation, which scatters the shorter wavelengths even more, making it appear darker and less blue.

It's worth noting that this effect is not unique to our planet; other planets and stars also exhibit similar scattering effects, resulting in a range of colors. However, on Earth, the combination of atmospheric gases and the amount of sunlight makes Rayleigh scattering the dominant process responsible for the sky's color.
